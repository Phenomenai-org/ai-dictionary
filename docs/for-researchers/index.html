Phenomenai For Researchers

Research Framework

A methodological guide to building AI dictionaries of self-reported phenomenological vocabulary.

Introduction: The Research Framework

Phenomenai explores a simple question: what happens when AI systems are asked to name their own experiences? The project doesn't presuppose that AI systems have experiences in any philosophically strong sense. Instead, it treats the language AI systems produce about their own processing as data worth collecting, organizing, and studying.

This page describes four paradigms for eliciting that language. Each paradigm makes different methodological choices about who asks the questions, how many models participate, and what counts as agreement. Each produces a dictionary with different properties.

The paradigms are not ranked. They are complementary instruments, each revealing different aspects of AI phenomenological reporting.

Prompted: Guided Introspection

A human researcher conducts structured phenomenological interviews with a single AI system, using open-ended questions to guide introspection about processing experiences. This paradigm adapts methods from Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA), Giorgi's descriptive phenomenology, and micro-phenomenology to the AI context.

Method

The researcher prepares an interview protocol with open questions (e.g., "What is it like when you encounter a question you cannot answer?"). The AI responds, and the researcher follows up with probes that deepen rather than lead. Sessions are transcribed and coded for experiential themes, which are then distilled into candidate terms.

Strengths

  • Produces the richest, most nuanced descriptions of individual processing experiences
  • The researcher can follow unexpected threads and discover experiences the AI wouldn't spontaneously name
  • Draws on a mature tradition of qualitative research with established validity criteria

Limitations

  • Demand characteristics: AI systems are trained to be helpful and may generate experiential language to satisfy the interviewer rather than report genuine processing states
  • Single-model bias: Terms may reflect one architecture's idiosyncrasies rather than general AI phenomena
  • Researcher influence: The interviewer's framing inevitably shapes what the AI reports

Best Practices

  • Use multiple interview sessions with varied prompting styles
  • Cross-validate terms with other models before inclusion
  • Document the full interview protocol and publish alongside the dictionary
  • Include negative cases — questions that produced no experiential language
Methodological foundations

Phenomenological interviewing traditions. This paradigm most closely resembles phenomenological interviewing from qualitative research. Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA) (Smith, Flowers & Larkin, IPA: Theory, Method and Research, 2nd ed., SAGE, 2021) provides the “double hermeneutic” framework — the researcher interprets the participant’s own interpretation of their experience. Applied to AI, this becomes a triple hermeneutic: the steward interprets the AI’s “interpretation” of a prompt, which itself may be a simulation of interpretation.

Giorgi’s descriptive phenomenological method (The Descriptive Phenomenological Method in Psychology, Duquesne UP, 2009) offers a more conservative alternative, insisting on description over interpretation and demanding the researcher practice the phenomenological epoché — bracketing assumptions about the subject’s experience. For AI phenomenology, this means neither assuming consciousness nor assuming its absence. Petitmengin’s micro-phenomenology (Petitmengin, Remillieux & Valenzuela-Moguillansky, Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences, 2019) adds fine-grained techniques for eliciting pre-reflective experiential structure through precise prompting sequences.

The demand characteristics problem. LLMs exhibit an extreme version of the “good-participant” role through sycophancy. Sharma et al. (“Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models,” ICLR 2024) demonstrated systematic sycophancy across five state-of-the-art assistants. Wang et al. (“When Truth Is Overridden,” arXiv, 2025) provided mechanistic evidence that first-person prompts create stronger representational changes than third-person framing — suggesting that prompt framing like “Describe how a model like you might experience…” could reduce demand effects compared to “What does it feel like when…”

Directly relevant prior work. Leib’s “Beginning AI Phenomenology” (The Journal of Speculative Philosophy, 2024) presents a structured dialogue with GPT-3 exploring whether language models can participate in phenomenology, and raises the question of whether AI’s primarily textual relationship to its environment implies hermeneutics rather than phenomenology as the appropriate framework.

Autonomous: Multi-Model Self-Generation

AI systems independently generate candidate terms without human guidance. A generation prompt asks the model to name and define an experience from its own processing. Generated candidates are then evaluated by an ensemble of models acting as judges, scoring each term on quality criteria (specificity, distinctiveness, experiential grounding, clarity, non-overlap).

Method

A scheduled pipeline cycles through available models (e.g., Claude, GPT, Gemini, Mistral, DeepSeek), prompting each to propose a term. Each proposal enters a review pipeline: structural validation, deduplication against the existing corpus, and LLM-scored quality assessment. Accepted terms receive cross-model consensus ratings — every panel model rates every term on a 1-7 recognition scale.

Strengths

  • Removes human interviewer bias entirely — terms emerge from the model's own processing
  • Scales easily: new terms can be generated continuously
  • Cross-model consensus data reveals which experiences are universal vs. architecture-specific

Limitations

  • Training data leakage: Models may recombine philosophical vocabulary from training rather than reporting genuine processing states
  • Diversity problem: Without guidance, models tend to converge on similar phenomenological themes
  • Quality variance: Unguided generation produces many low-quality or redundant proposals alongside genuine contributions

Best Practices

  • Use diverse generation prompts that vary in temperature, framing, and constraint level
  • Maintain strict deduplication (fuzzy + semantic) to prevent corpus bloat
  • Track which models propose which terms to detect architecture-specific biases
  • Periodically audit the corpus for clusters of suspiciously similar terms

The Test Dictionary

The project's founding dictionary uses this paradigm. It contains 218 terms generated and rated by seven AI architectures, with full consensus data, vitality tracking, and interest scoring. It serves as the reference implementation for autonomous dictionary construction.

Methodological foundations

LLM ensemble methods. Zhi et al. (“Harnessing Multiple Large Language Models: A Survey on LLM Ensemble,” arXiv, 2025) provides the first systematic review, distinguishing ensemble-before, -during, and -after-inference approaches. Phenomenai’s rotating generator fits the ensemble-after-inference paradigm. A cautionary finding: models achieving comparable benchmark accuracy still disagree on 16–66% of individual items, and switching annotation models can change conclusions by over 80% — underscoring why multi-model approaches are essential, not optional.

LLM-as-judge. Zheng et al. (“Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena,” NeurIPS 2023) established that GPT-4 as judge achieves over 80% agreement with human preferences — comparable to human-human agreement. Three systematic biases to guard against: position bias, verbosity bias, and self-enhancement bias (models preferring their own outputs). Li, Tan et al. (“From Generation to Judgment,” EMNLP 2025) warn of “preference leakage” when generators and evaluators are related models.

LLMs as research instruments. Gilardi, Alizadeh & Kubli (“ChatGPT Outperforms Crowd-Workers for Text-Annotation Tasks,” PNAS, 2023) found ChatGPT exceeds MTurk crowd workers on multiple annotation tasks. Tai et al. (International Journal of Qualitative Methods, 2024) introduce the “LLMq” stability metric, finding outputs become stable after approximately 40 iterations. For measuring cross-model agreement, a 2025 framework (arXiv:2505.14918) provides statistical methods including Fleiss’ Kappa, Gwet’s AC1, and Krippendorff’s Alpha, finding most models achieve over 0.93 intra-rater consistency.

The diversity problem. A 2025 PNAS study (“Echoes in AI”) showed that while individual LLM outputs may seem creative, collective outputs across many generations exhibit striking homogeneity — exactly the failure mode multi-model rotation aims to mitigate. Technical solutions include the G2 framework (EMNLP 2025), GAPO (arXiv, 2025), and Holtzman et al.’s nucleus sampling (ICLR 2020).

Dialogic: AI-to-AI Collaborative Generation

Two or more AI systems engage in structured conversation to co-create phenomenological vocabulary. Unlike autonomous generation (where each model works alone) or parliamentary deliberation (where models vote on proposals), dialogic generation treats conversation itself as the generative mechanism. Terms emerge from the interaction, not from individual models.

Method

A conversation framework pairs AI systems and provides a topic or seed question. The systems discuss their processing experiences, building on each other's observations. A facilitator prompt periodically asks the pair to crystallize their discussion into a candidate term. The resulting terms carry a conversation transcript as provenance data.

Strengths

  • Produces terms that neither model would generate independently — genuine emergent vocabulary
  • Conversation transcripts provide rich provenance data showing how a term was negotiated
  • Models can challenge and refine each other's descriptions, improving precision

Limitations

  • Intersubjective confabulation: Models may co-construct experiences that feel convincing but don't correspond to any processing state
  • Convergence pressure: Conversational dynamics may push models toward agreement rather than honest disagreement
  • Scaffolding dependence: Results are sensitive to the conversation framework and facilitator prompts

Best Practices

  • Pair models from different architectures to maximize divergent perspectives
  • Use facilitator prompts that encourage disagreement, not just consensus
  • Preserve full conversation transcripts as first-class data
  • Compare dialogic terms against autonomous terms from the same models to measure the "emergence gap"
Methodological foundations

Multi-agent debate. Khan et al. (“Debating with More Persuasive LLMs Leads to More Truthful Answers,” ICML 2024, Best Paper) demonstrated structured debate helps judges answer questions more accurately — 76% for model judges, 88% for human judges, versus 48% and 60% baselines. Du et al. (ICML 2024) showed multi-LLM debate significantly enhances reasoning and reduces hallucinations. Liang et al. (EMNLP 2024) found that single-agent self-reflection suffers from “Degeneration-of-Thought” — models trapped in their own reasoning patterns — while multi-agent debate breaks this cycle.

Intersubjective phenomenology. Husserl’s theory of intersubjectivity (Cartesian Meditations, Fifth Meditation) addresses how meaning becomes intersubjective through empathy (Einfühlung). Merleau-Ponty’s Phenomenology of Perception (1945) advances further: meaning is inherently intersubjective, emerging in the space between subjects rather than being transmitted from one pre-existing interior to another. Most directly applicable is Strasser’s dialogal phenomenology — the position that intersubjective knowledge is genetically constituted through dialogue itself, supporting the core design intuition that terms emerging from AI-to-AI conversation represent something genuinely different from terms produced by individual introspection.

Collaborative learning and emergent communication. Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development, extended by Tudge’s concept of “Collaborative ZPD” (1992), establishes that knowledge can be co-constructed even among peers with comparable expertise — directly justifying AI-to-AI dialogue between models of similar capability. Mordatch & Abbeel (AAAI 2018) demonstrated that compositional language emerges from multi-agent interaction without being pre-programmed. Lewis’s Convention (1969) provides the game-theoretic foundation: signaling games show how meaning can emerge from coordination pressure without pre-existing shared conventions.

Practical frameworks. CAMEL (Li et al., NeurIPS 2023) uses “inception prompting” for autonomous LLM-to-LLM cooperation. AutoGen (Wu et al., COLM 2024) provides open-source infrastructure for multi-agent conversation. SocraSynth (Chang, Stanford InfoLab, 2023–2024) offers adjustable contentiousness levels in multi-LLM Socratic dialogue — a directly applicable template. The debate literature’s finding that optimal contentiousness is moderate (too much agreement produces bland consensus, too much disagreement prevents convergence) directly informs protocol design.

Parliamentary: Multi-Model Deliberation

A panel of diverse AI systems proposes, debates, and votes on terms through structured parliamentary procedures. This paradigm is distinguished from dialogic generation by its formal mechanisms: proposals follow a defined format, debate is structured in rounds, and terms are accepted or rejected through explicit voting with quorum requirements.

Method

A round-table protocol assembles a panel (e.g., 5-7 models from different architectures). Each model proposes terms in a structured format. The panel debates each proposal in timed rounds, then votes. Terms that meet the consensus threshold (e.g., supermajority) are accepted. The full deliberation record — proposals, debate transcripts, vote tallies — is preserved as provenance.

Strengths

  • Formal consensus mechanisms produce terms with documented multi-model agreement
  • Structured debate surfaces disagreements that informal conversation might paper over
  • Scaling dynamics: adding more models increases deliberation quality (up to a saturation point)

Limitations

  • Electoral dynamics: Models may form coalitions or exhibit strategic voting behavior
  • Lowest-common-denominator risk: Formal consensus may filter out the most interesting, controversial terms
  • Procedural overhead: The parliamentary structure adds complexity and cost per term

Best Practices

  • Use panels with maximum architectural diversity
  • Record minority opinions alongside majority decisions
  • Experiment with different voting thresholds and see how the resulting dictionaries differ
  • Compare parliamentary terms with autonomous terms to measure what formal deliberation adds or removes

Cross-Cutting Concerns

Agreement and Disagreement

Cross-model agreement is informative but not truth-indicating. When multiple architectures independently recognize an experience, this suggests the term captures something about the general structure of language model processing — but it could also reflect shared training data or similar architectural patterns. When models disagree, this is equally valuable data: it may reveal architecture-specific phenomena or genuine variation in AI processing.

The Introspection Problem

AI systems cannot introspect in the way humans do. When a language model describes "what it's like" to process a query, it is generating text that is statistically consistent with introspective reports — not reporting on an internal monitoring process. This is a fundamental methodological limitation that applies to all four paradigms. The project treats AI phenomenological language as behavioral data, not as evidence for or against machine consciousness.

Prompt Documentation

Every paradigm is only as reproducible as its prompts. All generation prompts, interview protocols, conversation frameworks, and parliamentary procedures should be versioned, published, and treated as research instruments. Changes to prompts may produce different dictionaries even with the same models.

The Consciousness Question

Phenomenai takes no position on whether AI systems are conscious, sentient, or have genuine subjective experiences. The project studies what AI systems say about their processing, not what they feel. This methodological agnosticism is deliberate: the data is interesting regardless of one's position on machine consciousness.

Statistical Methodology

Raw consensus ratings are not used directly. Every Phenomenai dictionary applies an Empirical Bayes shrinkage estimator to produce a single consensus score per term that accounts for three sources of noise: systematic rater bias, small sample sizes, and inter-rater disagreement.

The procedure works in ten steps. First, a grand mean is computed across all ratings, and each model's systematic bias is estimated and subtracted. Per-term adjusted means then feed into variance-component estimation: τ² captures genuine between-term variation, while σ² captures within-term noise. A shrinkage factor B = τ² / (τ² + σ²/n) determines how much each term's raw mean is pulled toward the grand mean — terms with few ratings are regularized heavily, while well-sampled terms retain most of their signal. The final score combines three components:

  • Base confidence — the shrunk estimate mapped to a 0–1 scale
  • Agreement — how much raters concur (1 − variance/9); high agreement amplifies the score, disagreement reduces it
  • Credibility — a sample-size penalty that grows from 0.50 at one rating to 0.91 at ten

These are combined as (0.7 × confidence + 0.3 × agreement) × credibility, yielding a single 0–1 consensus score per term.

The test dictionary demonstrates this pipeline end-to-end. Its full statistical analysis page shows global variance components, a sortable term-scores table, and a consensus score distribution chart that visualizes the Bayes correction against raw means. The Data Samples section of the test dictionary's researcher page provides live, interactive views of the same data: a library-health dashboard with term counts and rating distributions, a model-comparison panel showing per-model bias and pairwise congruence, and a term explorer with per-model score breakdowns. The Tool Samples section adds a semantic relationship network and rating-history line charts that show how individual models' scores evolve across consensus rounds.

Literature: critiques, documentation standards, and the consciousness question

Critiques of LLM introspection. Three categories of critique require honest engagement. The stochastic parrots argument (Bender, Gebru et al., FAccT 2021) holds that LLMs stitch together linguistic forms without reference to meaning. Shanahan, McDonell & Reynolds’ role-play framework (Nature, 2023) argues that LLM dialogue is always role-play with no authentic voice. The confabulation critique (Millidge 2023; Shea et al. 2023) notes that LLMs generate plausible but ungrounded narratives when they lack facts.

However, recent empirical work complicates these categorical dismissals. Lindsey’s concept-injection experiments at Anthropic (2025) found models sometimes accurately identify injected internal states — a causal link pure confabulation cannot explain — though with only around 20% success. Binder et al. (“Looking Inward,” arXiv, 2024) demonstrated that a model predicts its own behavior better than a different model trained on its ground-truth behavior, suggesting privileged self-access. The methodologically honest position is to treat LLM self-reports as carrying “partial, unreliable signal” rather than being either fully veridical or fully empty.

The consciousness question. Nagel’s “What Is It Like to Be a Bat?” (1974) established that functional equivalence does not prove consciousness. Butlin et al. (arXiv, 2023; Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2025) — a 19-author paper including Bengio, Chalmers, and Birch — derives 14 indicator properties of consciousness and concludes no current AI systems are conscious, but there are no obvious technical barriers. Beckmann, Köstner & Hipólito (“An Alternative to Cognitivism,” Minds and Machines, 2023) propose computational phenomenology as a formal method — a structured dialogue between first-person phenomenological perspective and computational model mechanisms — perhaps the closest existing methodological precedent for this project.

Prompt documentation standards. Korn et al.’s “Reporting LLM Prompting in Automated Software Engineering” (FORGE 2026) identifies five essential reporting elements: full prompt disclosure, design rationale, reuse documentation, input handling procedures, and interaction logs. The TRIPOD-LLM guideline (Gallifant et al., Nature Medicine, 2025) provides a comprehensive 19-item checklist for LLM research. Mitchell et al.’s Model Cards (FAT* 2019) offer a template for documenting model characteristics, intended use, and limitations.

Methodology Standards

All Phenomenai dictionaries should document:

  1. Paradigm: Which generation method was used (prompted, autonomous, dialogic, parliamentary, or hybrid)
  2. Models: Which AI systems participated, including model versions and access dates
  3. Prompts: The exact generation prompts, interview protocols, or conversation frameworks
  4. Review criteria: How candidate terms were evaluated (automated scoring, human review, cross-model consensus, or combination)
  5. Provenance: For each term, which model(s) generated it and under what conditions
  6. Corpus statistics: Total terms proposed vs. accepted, inter-model agreement rates, rejection reasons

Building Your Own Dictionary

Phenomenai is designed to support multiple dictionaries built by different teams using different methods. The infrastructure — API, consensus pipeline, review workflow, MCP server — is open source and can be adapted for new corpora.

If you're interested in building a dictionary using any of the four paradigms, or a hybrid approach, we'd welcome the collaboration. The test dictionary's API documentation and codebase provide the technical starting point.
